We Solved the Speed Problem. We Created a Clarity Problem.

AI made building faster. That should mean more learning, more testing, more iteration. Instead, most teams are producing more noise, faster. The problem isn't capability. It's clarity.

We Solved the Speed Problem. We Created a Clarity Problem.

Ethan Mollick shared research this week where he gave a group of financial professionals access to Claude to help with a complex valuation task. The AI was genuinely capable but the people still struggled. Not because of what the AI couldn't do. Because of how it communicated. Giant walls of text. Tangents they didn't ask for. Sprawling back-and-forth that created more cognitive overhead than it removed.

The chatbot interface was the obstacle. Not the work.

I've been running this through my mind alongside two other things I read this week. Marty Cagan wrote about the difference between building to learn and building to earn. His point: when building gets cheaper, teams that still treat every build like a revenue bet are leaving discovery on the table. And Fabricio Teixeira wrote about vibe coding and what it's doing to design authority. The line that caught my attention: "something that looks finished" is not "something that is finished."

Then there's Teresa Torres writing about Todoist Ramble this week. I use that feature almost every day. The reason it works is exactly what she named: they didn't use AI to build faster. They used it to solve a specific friction point. Capture tasks while you're in motion. Parse intent from voice. Ship something small and correct. That pattern matters more than the feature.

Here's what connects all of it.

AI has genuinely dropped the cost of building. That should unlock more learning, faster iteration, better discovery. And in the right hands, it does. But most of the interfaces weren't designed for the actual work. They were built to demonstrate capability. So instead of faster clarity, we get faster confusion. More output. Less signal.

We solved the speed problem. And in doing so, we created a clarity problem.

This matters because Shreyas Doshi is right: in an AI-native world, the scarce resource isn't execution. It's judgment. Knowing what to build. Knowing when "looks done" actually means "is done." You cannot develop judgment in an environment that overwhelms you with noise.

I know what it's like to see busy outputs that feel like progress when they're not. I've even fallen into that trap myself several times.

The real work right now is designing for clarity, not capability. Interfaces that surface the right information at the right moment. Tools that guide judgment instead of flooding it.

Building faster toward confusion isn't the future. Designing clearly for it is.
